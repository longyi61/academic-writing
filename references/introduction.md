# Writing the introduction

The introduction is where reviewers decide whether to trust the paper. Top OM/management journals converge on a remarkably stable shape, visible across analytical and empirical work alike. This file gives that shape and the craft moves that fill it. Apply it when drafting or restructuring an introduction; for sentence-level prose, the main SKILL.md still governs.

## The six moves, in order

A strong introduction walks the reader from the real world to the paper's answer without a visible seam. The sequence:

1. **Open on a phenomenon, not a gap.** The first sentence states something true about the world, then grounds it immediately in named firms and hard numbers. "When sellers launch new products, consumers often struggle to assess their value…" then Chanel's 2020 N°5 video with "the perfume appearing for just two seconds." "Roughly 70% of the world's cocoa beans are produced by smallholder farmers who… live on less than two U.S. dollars per day." "60% of rural India lacks internet access." Do **not** open on "the literature has not examined…" — the gap comes later and quietly. A reviewer should feel the problem is real before they're told it's unstudied.

2. **Turn the phenomenon into a puzzle.** Out of the facts, surface a tension that isn't obviously resolvable. Firms *could* disclose product information cheaply but many choose silence. A flexible premium designed to help farmers *can* leave them worse off. Booking agents extend a platform's reach *but* add operational complexity and a third mouth to feed. The puzzle is what licenses a model: if the answer were obvious, there'd be nothing to solve.

3. **State research questions, and earn each one.** Make the questions explicit — often as literal interrogatives — and have each grow out of a motivating sentence rather than appear by decree. "These observations motivate our final research questions: Under self-labeling, would a firm prefer a flexible premium over a fixed premium?" "Together, these considerations motivate our first research question: in the presence of booking agents, how should platforms set the price and wages…?" One question is fine; several are fine if each is individually motivated and individually answered later. The tell of a weak intro is a question that arrives unearned.

4. **Preview the model in one move.** A single paragraph naming the apparatus and its key primitives — "To study this, we analyze the joint information disclosure and pricing strategy of a monopolistic seller…", "we develop a game-theoretic model that involves an NGO certifier…". Name the few modeling choices that matter (objective vs. subjective attributes, search cost, limited inventory) and stop. The full setup belongs to Section 2 or 3.

5. **Preview findings with direction *and* condition, counterintuitive one first.** This is the payload. State the actual sign of each result and the boundary condition *in the same breath*, and lead with the result that surprises:
   - "contrary to expectation, we show that the flexible premium approach can lead to a lower expected farmer income… when the expected yield of the crop is low or the yield variability is high."
   - "the seller should conceal (disclose) attribute information when the search cost is low (high)."
   - "whereas conventional platforms often pair surge pricing with increased provider wages, we show that platforms with booking agents may instead… increase booking agent wages and decrease provider wages."

   A vague preview ("we derive several insights about disclosure") wastes the highest-value paragraph in the paper. Enumerate with First / Second / Finally so the findings map one-to-one onto the questions. If a result runs against a known result in the field, say so — that positions the contribution without the word "novel."

6. **Quarantine novelty to the literature positioning.** Keep "novel," "first," and "to the best of our knowledge" *out* of the intro's findings preview. The intro sells through the surprise of the results; the gap-and-first language lives in a Related Literature subsection, organized by two or three streams, each closing with one sentence of "our contribution": "Our contributions to this literature are threefold. First, we… Second, we… Third, we…" or a single "We contribute to this literature by…". Selling via surprise up front and via positioning later is the division of labor these journals expect; doing the reverse (self-praise in the intro, results buried) reads as a weaker paper.

A short roadmap paragraph may follow, though many recent papers drop it and go straight to the literature section.

## Craft moves that fill the template

- **Earn the question, then ask it.** Every research question is preceded by the wrinkle that makes it nontrivial — yield uncertainty, an online channel, surplus redistribution. The rhythm is *complication → question*, never a bare question.

- **Plant one sticky handle.** Give the central mechanism a short, memorable name in the intro and reuse it everywhere: *diamond-in-the-rough* consumers, the *win–win–win* outcome, *booking agents*, *fixed vs. flexible premium*. The label does the work of a paragraph each time it recurs, and reviewers cite the paper by it. (See "Define, then reuse — and name your mechanisms" in SKILL.md.)

- **Thread a real firm or partner through.** When there's an industry anchor, don't spend it once. Hello Tractor opens the motivation, grounds the model's assumptions, and returns at the end of the intro as an impact note: "Our findings have informed changes in Hello Tractor's practice." That model-to-practice loop is strong evidence of relevance.

- **Foreground counterintuition explicitly.** Mark the surprising result and give it room — "Surprisingly," "contrary to expectation," "This counterintuitive finding, to the best of our knowledge, has not been explored." The reversal is usually the reason the paper exists; the institutional setup before it should be built precisely to make the reversal land.

- **Micro-signpost the intuition.** A short connective like "The rationale is as follows." opening a mechanism explanation lets each findings paragraph explain *why*, not just *what* — the same claim → support → why → link shape the main file prescribes, compressed into the preview.

- **Carry boundary conditions in the preview sentence.** "only in settings with ample certified supply relative to demand," "when agents' demand aggregation cost is moderate." Previewing *when* as well as *what* signals the comparative-statics spine of the analysis before any equation appears, and it reads as precision rather than hedging.

## Quick check before moving on

- Does the first sentence describe the world, or the literature? It should be the world.
- Is every research question preceded by a sentence that makes it non-obvious?
- Does the findings preview give the *sign* of each result and the *condition* under which it holds?
- Is the counterintuitive result first, and marked as surprising?
- Are "novel/first/to the best of our knowledge" absent from the intro and present only in the literature positioning?
- Is there a single coined handle for the central mechanism, used in both the abstract and the intro?
